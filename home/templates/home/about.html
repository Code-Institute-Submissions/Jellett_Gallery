About

Jellett, Madonna of Eire, oil on canvas, (1941)

Jellett Gallery

The year was 1923 and on returning to Dublin Mainie Jellett submitted a cubist and daring work, titled "Decoration" to the the annual Society of Dublin Painters' exhibition. It was an explosion of overlapping color fields, with a religious icon as centre-piece. It was modern, strident, and loudly cubist. Queue the critical firestorm. The art reviewer for The Irish Times was baffled, writing the work was nonsense that: “may, to the man who understands the most up-to-date modern art, mean something; but to me they presented an insoluble mess.” Prominent art critic George Russell called Jellett “a late victim to Cubism in some sub-section of this artistic malaria.” Another critic referred to the piece as 'sub-human art.'

Of course, she had touched a critical nerve but this piece would go on to hold place of honor at the National Gallery. The critical reversal was relatively swift as four yeats later the Irish Times heralded Jellett as “the only serious exponent in this country of the ultra-modernist school of painting.” For the next two decades, Jellett created work that would define art in Ireland. She would be shown in the Irish section of the exhibition at the 1928 Olympic Games.

Jellett, Head of a Woman, oil on canvas, (1934)

Jellett co-founded the Irish Exhibition of Living Art, a celebration of unconventional Irish artists. She wrote in "An Approach To Paining", about her desire to represent the spirit of her country and its people:

“The idea of an artist being a special person, an exotic flower set apart from other people is one of the errors resulting from the industrial revolution, and the fact of artists being pushed out of their lawful position in the life and society of the present day. … Their present enforced isolation from the majority is a very serious situation and I believe it is one of the many causes which has resulted in the present chaos we live in. The art of a nation is one of the ultimate facts by which its spiritual health is judged and appraised by posterity.”

Who We Are

The Jellett Gallery is a space dedicated to supporting young female artists. We are a commercial gallery run operated as collective by the artists themselves. When an objective critique was applied to the art world clear systematic discrimintaion was present. The experiences of Mainie Jellett deeply informed the creation of this collective, the individual discrimination she faced would be challenged by collective struggle.

Jellett, The Bells, oil on canvas, (1940)

The gallery does not, however, supporting any particular ideology or arts practice. It is a space which sought and found a place within Ireland’s vibrant art culture to represent female artists. We make no apologies, give no critiques, write no thesis, the work will have to speak for itself. Member selection, exhibition curation and hanging, invitations and openings are undertaken by the collective committee.
